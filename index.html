Brief walkthroughs of select published projects

The Month Coronavirus Felled American Business
 | April 2020

To immediately convey the sudden rise of coronavirus cases in the U.S. and the rapidity with which the American business landscape was forced to deal with the pandemic, we used a full-screen, scroll-driven bar chart representing the number of confirmed cases as an impactful introduction to the story.

Dotting the story were calendrical subheads, or guide posts, to help the reader keep track of key economic and virus metrics as the story progressed chronologically.

The Credit Score Game
 | October 2019

The idea of gamifying the project came up early in the discussion as an effective and engaging way for readers to gauge their credit-score knowledge and to demystify the "whys" behind credit-score dips and rises. We needed to cater to as broad of a population, while preserving profile specificity to be able to offer helpful information. In the end, we offered five profiles with scores across the spectrum and five unique sets of questions and responses.

To provide summarized insights for the reader, the game's backend — built using Vuex — kept track of guesses and their accuracy.

A Decade of News: How the Big Stories Evolved
 | December 2019

The results of a WSJ analysis of articles published in the 2010s were the stars of the project. The user's scrolling drove when phrases were highlighted and context was provided. The simple column animation provides a sense of the narratives unfolding as the years progress. I developed this in Vue.js — after using the framework for the first time in "The Credit-Score Game," I found every chance to leverage its responsiveness.

Is it Worth Investing in Unprofitable Companies? We Ran the Numbers

 | March 2020

Generously sprinkled with sentences that referenced calculations and derived insights, the initial story draft seemed to have a lot of capacity for graphics that would effectively and clearly convey those insights — particularly to someone, like me, with little knowledge of investing.

So, after identifying the story's key points, I distilled a dataset of the returns of all companies listed on the NYSE and Nasdaq from 2015-19 into three visualizations on profitability and returns, volatility and interest-rate risk.

In Valuing Colleges, Tops in Prestige Doesn’t Always Mean Tops in Starting Salaries

 | May 2020

I produced the datasets used in the graphic by using Python and pandas to manipulate results obtained from querying the U.S. Department of Education's College Scorecard Data API. This graphic was initially prototyped as an interactive tool in which users could compare earnings across public, private, selective and nonselective schools, across schools and across majors. We settled on focusing on several key examples that showed program matters more than prestige.

The visualization's triangular design — coded in d3.js — functions as an overview of earnings distribution and rankings and offers a look at how prestige, or selectivity, impacts earnings.
